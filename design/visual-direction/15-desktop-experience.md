# PAYLIO VISUAL DIRECTION SHEET 15

# Desktop Experience

### A Financial Workspace

---

## Purpose

The desktop experience extends Paylio beyond the hand.

On a larger canvas, the product should not simply stretch.

It should breathe.

The desktop environment gives Paylio room to demonstrate its full visual language.

Generous space.

Deep layouts.

Refined hierarchy.

A workspace that earns the word premium.

---

## Emotional Objective

The desktop experience should create:

Command.

Clarity.

Presence.

Confidence.

Focus.

Users should feel they have entered a private financial workspace.

Built entirely for them.

---

## Story

Imagine sitting in the private office of an extraordinarily successful person.

The desk is clear.

The light is controlled.

The layout is intentional.

Every object has a place.

Nothing is decorative.

The environment communicates that serious decisions are made here.

Paylio's desktop experience should create that feeling.

The moment a user opens the product on a large screen,

they should feel that this was designed for exactly this moment.

---

## Design Philosophy

More space does not mean more content.

It means more breathing room.

Desktop gives the product permission to be expansive.

Not crowded.

Not compensating.

Simply more present.

---

## Layout Architecture

The sidebar occupies the left.

220 pixels wide.

Fixed.

Permanent.

The main content area fills the remainder.

Maximum content width 768 pixels on most pages.

Centered where appropriate.

Left-anchored where content demands it.

The rest is space.

Intentional.

Valuable.

---

## The Sidebar

The sidebar is the anchor of the desktop experience.

It should feel like the spine of the product.

Paylio logo at the top.

Return link to the marketing page.

Navigation.

User identity card at the bottom.

The sidebar communicates that this is a persistent workspace.

Not a page being visited.

A place being inhabited.

---

## Navigation

Desktop navigation lives in the sidebar.

Items appear as refined horizontal links.

Icon left.

Label right.

Active state receives subtle teal background.

Active icon illuminates.

Inactive items remain quiet.

Navigation should feel like a curated menu.

Not a list of links.

---

## Content Hierarchy

The desktop layout follows a different visual rhythm than mobile.

Less vertical stacking.

More contextual grouping.

Information breathes horizontally as well as vertically.

But breathing room should never be confused with emptiness.

Every centimeter of space should feel considered.

---

## The Balance Card on Desktop

On desktop, the balance card should feel even more like a premium object.

Slightly larger.

More generous padding.

Deeper materials.

The surrounding space makes it feel displayed rather than embedded.

Like a product in an exhibition.

---

## Cursor Interactions

The cursor becomes part of the experience on desktop.

Hovering over cards gently lifts them.

Hovering over glass surfaces creates subtle reflective response.

Hovering near the balance card causes ambient light to breathe.

The environment acknowledges presence.

Nothing dramatic.

Everything considered.

---

## Hover States

Every interactive element deserves a refined hover state.

Gentle elevation.

Subtle surface illumination.

Edge highlight.

Cursor change.

Hover states communicate that something is possible.

They should create quiet anticipation.

Never aggression.

---

## Information Density

Desktop allows more information to coexist comfortably.

But restraint remains the governing principle.

Do not add information because space exists.

Add information because it serves understanding.

Every additional data point must earn its presence.

---

## Multi-Column Where Justified

Some pages benefit from multiple columns on desktop.

Insights.

Security settings.

Cards and their controls.

But multi-column layout should emerge from content logic.

Never from a desire to fill space.

The grid serves the content.

The content does not serve the grid.

---

## Atmosphere

The desktop experience should feel like entering a premium banking environment.

Dark.

Quiet.

Spacious.

The sidebar glows softly.

The content area breathes.

Ambient particles drift across the background.

The product feels architectural.

A space with walls, depth, and light.

Not a page on a browser.

---

## Sidebar User Card

The user identity card at the bottom of the sidebar matters.

The user should feel recognized every time they look at it.

Their name.

Their initials rendered as a beautiful avatar.

Their account type.

Premium.

Personal.

Present.

---

## Cinematography

Imagine a film crew setting up to shoot a desktop banking application.

Wide lens.

Low camera angle.

The sidebar fills one side of the frame.

The content breathes on the other.

Soft teal reflections move across the display.

The ambient environment glows behind the glass.

The product looks like architecture.

Not software.

---

## Things to Avoid

Do not stretch mobile layouts to fill the desktop.

Do not use the sidebar as a decoration.

Do not add widgets simply because space allows.

Do not create horizontal scrolling.

Do not reduce the sidebar on medium screens unless absolutely necessary.

Do not make the desktop experience feel like a website.

Do not sacrifice the sidebar's presence.

It is not navigation.

It is identity.

---

## Quality Standard

The desktop experience should feel as though it were designed by a dedicated team working exclusively on this format.

No component should feel adapted.

Every layout should feel native.

Every hover state should feel crafted.

Every pixel of negative space should feel earned.

The desktop Paylio should be as remarkable as the mobile Paylio.

Different canvas.

Same craftsmanship.

Design the desktop experience accordingly.

Give it gravity.

Give it presence.
