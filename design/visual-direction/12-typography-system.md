# PAYLIO VISUAL DIRECTION SHEET 12

# Typography System

### The Voice of Money

---

## Purpose

Typography is not how Paylio looks.

Typography is how Paylio speaks.

Every number communicates financial reality.

Every headline communicates confidence.

Every label communicates clarity.

Typography must earn trust before users engage with a single interaction.

Words should feel chosen.

Not generated.

---

## Emotional Objective

Typography should create:

Confidence.

Clarity.

Authority.

Calm.

Precision.

Users should feel informed the moment they read.

Never confused.

Never overwhelmed.

---

## Story

Imagine a financial newspaper from thirty years in the future.

The paper no longer exists.

Only the typography remains.

It is precise.

Architectural.

Quiet.

The headlines command attention without shouting.

The numbers feel like objects.

The body text disappears into understanding.

The reader absorbs information without effort.

That is the reading experience Paylio should create.

---

## Design Philosophy

Typography should reduce effort.

Not create it.

Users should never need to search for what matters.

Hierarchy should be immediately obvious.

The most important information should dominate.

Everything else quietly supports.

---

## The Typeface

Plus Jakarta Sans.

Geometric without feeling cold.

Confident without feeling aggressive.

Premium without feeling corporate.

It possesses the quiet intelligence that defines Paylio.

Every weight serves a role.

Nothing is interchangeable.

---

## Type Scale

### Balance Figures

The largest type in the application.

44 pixels minimum on mobile.

Larger on desktop.

Extrabold weight.

Negative letter spacing.

Tabular numerals.

The balance should feel like architecture.

Not text.

---

### Primary Headlines

Page titles.

Section anchors.

Bold.

Comfortable size.

Tight letter spacing.

Generous line height.

Headlines should arrive with confidence.

---

### Secondary Headlines

Card labels.

Group titles.

Transaction categories.

Semibold.

Slightly smaller.

Still commanding.

---

### Body Text

Supporting information.

Descriptions.

Contextual detail.

Regular weight.

Comfortable size.

Never compressed.

Always legible.

---

### Labels

Dates.

Timestamps.

Category names.

Secondary amounts.

Small.

Quiet.

Uppercase with generous tracking.

Labels should whisper.

Never compete.

---

### Micro Text

Status indicators.

Metadata.

Supporting footnotes.

The quietest type.

Exists to clarify.

Never to decorate.

---

## Numbers

Numbers are the most important typographic element in Paylio.

They should feel architectural.

Every digit precisely aligned.

Tabular numerals always active.

No digit should shift position as values change.

Large balances should command the page.

Small amounts should remain dignified.

Money deserves typographic respect.

---

## Spacing

Spacing is as important as the text itself.

Every line needs room to breathe.

Headlines need generous margin below.

Paragraphs need comfortable leading.

Labels need air above and below.

Whitespace is not empty.

It is silence.

And silence communicates calm.

---

## Weight Hierarchy

Extrabold for balance figures.

Bold for page headlines.

Semibold for section headers and card labels.

Medium for important body content.

Regular for standard body text.

Light is not used.

Every weight communicates position.

Nothing is accidental.

---

## Color in Typography

Primary text is pure white.

Secondary text fades to sixty percent opacity.

Muted text reaches thirty-five percent.

Teal text marks interactive elements and positive values.

Red text appears only for genuine financial problems.

Never for decoration.

Typography color should communicate state.

Not style.

---

## Alignment

Left-aligned by default.

Balance figures and primary amounts can center within their containers.

Center alignment should never apply to large bodies of text.

Right alignment belongs to financial values in lists.

Alignment should always serve readability.

Never aesthetics.

---

## Motion

Typography should never change abruptly.

Balance figures count naturally when values change.

Numbers tick upward or downward like a premium counter.

Page transitions carry text smoothly.

Labels fade into place.

Nothing jumps.

Typography should move as though it has weight.

---

## Cinematography

Imagine filming a single perfectly typeset balance.

Extreme macro.

The digits fill the frame.

Soft teal light travels across the letterforms.

Shadows reveal the micro texture beneath.

The numbers feel three-dimensional.

Almost physical.

That is the typographic presence Paylio should achieve.

---

## Things to Avoid

Do not mix typefaces.

Do not use light weight for body text.

Do not use uppercase for paragraphs.

Do not compress line height.

Do not reduce letter spacing arbitrarily.

Do not use type as decoration.

Do not over-style numbers.

Do not sacrifice legibility for aesthetics.

Typography should communicate.

Always.

---

## Quality Standard

Every typeset screen should pass a single test.

Could this typography appear in a luxury financial publication?

If the answer is no,

the hierarchy needs refinement.

The spacing needs attention.

The weight needs adjustment.

Typography is the most used element in the entire product.

It must be the most refined.

## Design Principle for Claude Code

When setting type, never ask how it looks.

Ask what it communicates.

A balance that feels too small communicates insecurity.

A label that feels too large communicates noise.

Hierarchy is not about making things bigger.

It is about making the right things matter.

Typography done correctly becomes invisible.

Users only remember the understanding it created.
